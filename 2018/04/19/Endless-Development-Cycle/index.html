Endless Development Cycle

Tanner Phan
19-Apr-2018

In March 2018, I attended the SeattleJS monthly MeetUp in downtown Seattle. Normally I would avoid traveling to downtown because of traffic and it’s always a hassle to find parking, but since someone was giving a talk on TypeScript so I decided to put on paints and leave the house. This was one of those times where I try not to think and just do and the end result was very positive.

I met an older gentleman named Mark during the event. Mark looks like he could be my father; I knew he would have a lot to share and I was right. Mark worked 15 years with several tech giants in Seattle. Now, he is working in construction. He pointed out the slow evolving nature of construction, and he thinks there are a lot of opportunities for innovations there. I totally agreed with Mark and thought he was brilliant.

With more trust in Mark, I wanted to get his advice on developer productivity.

In the early days, I was doing designs with HTML and CSS. This ended up taking a lot of time and my design abilities were limited because I was using both sides of the brain simultaneously—solving logic problems and thinking in design. I eventually buckled down and learned to design with Sketch Design, and I learned to create artifacts with Gimp. Sure enough, in no time, I began to see improvement in my designs. My workflow also improved, and I was feeling very happy and productive.

Up until a week before attending the MeetUp, when I felt completely exhausted. Almost like how I was, doing designs with HTML and CSS. I had a backlog on Trello that felt like it was never going to be finished. With 15 years in tech, I hoped that Mark would be the one to talk to about developer productivity. After hearing me out, he responded: “You need discipline.” Pausing for a long second, then Mark went on to explain further. During that moment, I thought to myself “More discipline?”. I tried my best not think anymore and focused on Mark talk. He explained to me the importance of taking small breaks when working and keeping a healthy active routine. It made sense to me because we evolved from hunting and gathering people. Our physiology doesn’t allow us to sit 12 hours a day for months on.

Before we were able to finish, the MeetUp talk had resumed and we went back to our seats. On the way home that night, I thought about Mark’s remark of needing discipline. Even though the physiology reasoning didn’t pertain to me because I was going to the gym regularly. In fact, I look forward to going to the gym because I would get to socialize and meet with friends. And twice a day, I take breaks to take our dog, Charlie, out before picking my sister from school in the afternoon. Staying active and taking small breaks were really not my problems. Yet, I was still thinking about “discipline”, then I realized there is another aspect to my situation with the endless backlog. My problem has to do with feature/design creeping. I made the mistake of falling into the loop of design and code and sometimes, adding more features in the middle of the development cycle. I realized it’s demoralizing to work on something for a long time and feeling like you’ve accomplished nothing.

When working on big projects by yourself, you’re more prone to make this mistake of falling into the endless development cycle. This can be very detrimental on your morale. To avoid this, you need to be clear on what needs to be accomplished in each development cycle, and you need to be disciplined not to add, or modify, things in the middle of a cycle. Also, don’t hold back on celebrating successes. Celebrating small milestones helps boosting morale; And every little boost you get will contribute to making a better product, better code, and ultimately a happier you!
